\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Introduction}

Agriculture is very important for Bangladeshs economy. It helps with food security, jobs and rural livelihoods.. Farmers, especially small ones in rural areas face many challenges. They struggle to use technologies and get timely information. They do not know much about crop diseases. They also face fluctuating market prices, unpredictable weather and limited access to buyers. These problems reduce their productivity and profitability.

To solve these problems we introduce AgriGenie. It is an agriculture assistance platform. It combines services into one easy-to-use system. AgriGenie has crop disease detection, weather support, market price forecasting and a farmer-buyer marketplace. It promotes precision agriculture and sustainable farming.

Unlike tools that are expensive or limited AgriGenie is affordable and multifunctional. It uses web technologies and artificial intelligence. It helps achieve the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal (SDG 2: Zero Hunger).

\section{Problem Statement}

Bangladeshs agriculture sector has problems many problems. Some of them are given below:

\begin{enumerate}
	
	\item \textbf{Disease Management:} Farmers lack tools to detect crop diseases early. They use many chemicals, which harm the yield and environment.
	
	\item \textbf{Price Information:} Farmers do not have access to market prices. Intermediaries take advantage of them.
	
	\item \textbf{Weather Forecasting:} Farmers struggle to predict weather extremes like floods and droughts. This leads to crop losses.
	
	\item \textbf{Market Access:} Small farmers cannot sell directly to buyers. This reduces their profitability.
	
	\item \textbf{Knowledge Gap:} Farmers do not have access to best practices and extension services.
	
\end{enumerate}

These challenges reduce crop quality, profitability and resilience. AgriGenie aims to solve these issues with a technology-driven solution.

\section{Project Objectives}

The objectives of AgriGenie are:

\begin{itemize}
	
	\item To develop an AI-powered tool to identify crop diseases from images.
	
	\item To implement a model for price prediction and trend analysis.
	
	\item To provide a farmer-buyer marketplace to reduce intermediaries.
	
	\item To ensure accessibility with dashboards and intuitive interfaces.
	
	\item To design a system for large-scale adoption.
	
	\item To optimize for devices to reach rural farmers.
	
	\item Incorporate authentication and encryption to protect user data.
	
\end{itemize}

\section{Project Scope}

AgriGenie is a platform that helps farmers in Bangladesh. It has features that are already working well. For example farmers, buyers and administrators can log in securely. See only the information that is relevant to them. The platform uses intelligence to detect diseases in crops like potatoes, tomatoes and peppers. It can identify 15 diseases and even suggest how to treat them. AgriGenie also has a marketplace where farmers can sell their crops directly to buyers. This helps farmers get a price for their crops.

The platform also has a feature that allows farmers and buyers to chat with each other in time. They can share pictures of their crops. Discuss prices. AgriGenie can even predict what crop prices will be like in the 30 days. It can also warn farmers about weather like floods, droughts and heat waves. This helps farmers take steps to protect their crops.

From a point of view AgriGenie uses modern technology to make sure it can handle a lot of users. The backend is built using Django and the frontend uses Bootstrap. The platform uses intelligence and machine learning to detect diseases and predict prices. It also uses APIs to get weather data and authenticate users.

\section{Novelty and Innovation}

AgriGenie is different from other agricultural platforms because it offers a complete solution. It helps farmers detect diseases, get market information and sell their crops directly to buyers. The platform uses models that're specific to Bangladesh. This means it can detect diseases that're common in this region more accurately.

AgriGenie is also designed to be easy to use. The dashboards are simple.It can be used on mobile phones. This makes it easy for farmers with technical knowledge to use the platform. The platform also allows farmers and buyers to communicate with each other in time. This helps them negotiate prices and make deals without needing intermediaries.

AgriGenie can even be used offline. This means farmers in areas can still use some of the features even if they do not have internet access. Overall AgriGenie is a platform that addresses many of the challenges faced by farmers in Bangladesh and South Asia. It helps farmers detect diseases predict prices and get information about the weather. This makes it an useful tool for farmers in this region. AgriGenie is a platform in the field of smart farming. It is a digital platform that integrates multiple modules to support farmers in Bangladesh.


\section{Unfamiliarity of the Problem \& Solution}

Most agricultural platforms are either specialized or expensive. They are inaccessible to smallholder farmers in Bangladesh. Current solutions lack integration between crop disease management, market intelligence, weather alerts and direct farmer-buyer communication.

AgriGenie combines these features into a system tailored to Bangladesh. It uses datasets for disease detection and integrates price prediction using genetic algorithms.

\chapter{Related Works}

\section{Overview of Agricultural Technology Platforms}

The agricultural technology sector has seen a lot of growth in the few years. Many digital platforms have come up to help farmers manage diseases, get access to markets and make decisions.. Most of these solutions are either very specialized or only work in certain areas, which means they do not work very well in Bangladesh. This part of the book looks at some of the known platforms and systems talks about what they are good and bad at and shows the problems that AgriGenie is trying to solve. AgriGenie is trying to fill the gap that exists in the agricultural technology sector in Bangladesh. The agricultural technology sector needs something like AgriGenie to help the farmers, in Bangladesh.

\section{Disease Detection and Diagnosis Systems}

\subsection{PlantVillage Platform}

PlantVillage is a known platform. It helps farmers upload pictures of their crops to get help with disease diagnosis. Disease experts in farming then look at these pictures. Give advice according to that. These experts are good at sharing knowledge. However PlantVillage has some problems. It takes a time to get answers because real people have to look at each picture. PlantVillage does not have a way to automatically detect problems in time. Another issue is that the pictures and data used to train PlantVillage are not specifically about crops grown in Bangladesh. This makes it less useful for farmers in Bangladesh. The platform relies on experts for disease diagnosis. These experts provide feedback and recommendations to farmers.

PlantVillage is used for knowledge dissemination. However it needs to improve its support, for Bangladeshi crops.

\subsection{AI-Based Detection Systems}

People have been studying how artificial intelligence can help find diseases in plants. For example Kumar and his team used a kind of computer program called convolutional neural networks to figure out what was wrong with crops. They were able to do this about 94\% of the time. This is a start but these programs were only tested with information from India and they do not work well in places with limited resources. Artificial intelligence for plant disease detection is still a thing. Most systems that use intelligence for plant disease detection only look at finding the disease and do not tell farmers what to do to make the plants healthy again or work with the other tools that farmers use to take care of their farms. Artificial intelligence for plant disease detection needs to be able to do more, than just detect diseases.

\subsection{Limitations in Existing Approaches}

Existing disease detection platforms face several limitations. Such as:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Lack of training on crops and diseases prevalent in Bangladesh.
	\item Absence of treatment recommendations alongside detection.
	\item No integration with weather data or farm management systems.
	\item Limited adaptability to diverse leaf types and image quality in rural settings.
\end{itemize}

AgriGenie addresses these gaps by detecting 15 plant diseases across potato, tomato, and pepper crops, providing confidence scores, classifying disease types (fungal, bacterial, viral, pest) and offering treatment recommendations. It also integrates disease history tracking and weather-aware alerts, making it more comprehensive than existing systems.

\section{Agricultural Price Prediction Systems}

\subsection{Agri-market Platforms}

There are platforms like Indias e-NAM that gather and share price information. However they do not have the ability to predict what will happen in the future. Some people like Harsha have used models called ARIMA to try and guess what crop prices will be. They were able to get pretty close with an error of 8.2\%.. These models have a hard time dealing with big changes in the market and unexpected events. Indias e-NAM and other platforms like it need to be able to handle these surprises to really help people understand what will happen with crop prices.

\subsection{Machine Learning Approaches}

Akter and others compared three methods for predicting prices: ARIMA, exponential smoothing and LSTM neural networks. They found that LSTM models made mistakes 6.5\% of the time.. These methods need a lot of data and computer power, which many small farmers do not have.

AgriGenie is different. It uses an algorithm to predict crop prices for the next 30 days. It also finds trends in price changes and tells farmers when to sell. AgriGenie uses price data and shows trends with charts making it easy for farmers to understand and use the predictions.

\section{Agricultural Marketplaces}

\subsection{Direct-to-Consumer Platforms}

There are platforms like Direct2Farm and FarmDrive in Africa. These platforms let farmers sell their produce directly to the people who will eat it. This is a thing because it helps cut out the people in the middle.. Direct2farm and FarmDrive have some problems. They have to figure out how to make sure product quality. They also have to make sure they can work in different areas.. They have to find a way to help farmers and buyers trust each other when they use Direct2Farm and FarmDrive.

\subsection{Challenges in Existing Marketplaces}

Current marketplaces have some issues. They often lack:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Mechanisms for crop quality verification.
	\item Coverage across diverse geographic regions.
	\item Real-time communication between farmers and buyers.
	\item Trust-building features such as ratings and reviews.
\end{enumerate}

AgriGenie addresses these challenges by integrating a farmer–buyer marketplace with quality grading (A, B, C), buyer ratings and real-time WebSocket-based chat. This ensures transparency, trust and fair pricing.

\section{Comprehensive Agricultural Platforms}

\subsection{ICRISAT’s Digital Advisory Services}

ICRISAT gives farmers advice by sending them messages on their phones. This helps farmers a bit.. Icrisat only does this in some areas. Also farmers cannot talk back to ICRISAT through these messages.. The way ICRISAT does, is not very easy for people to use who are used to modern technology. ICRISAT services are just not very good for farmers who like to use their phones and computers a lot.

\subsection{Regional Context: Bangladesh-Specific Systems}

Bangladesh does not have agricultural technology platforms yet. Most solutions available now are taken from other countries. They do not think about the crops we grow here or how we grow them or even if they can be used in our language. This means we need a solution that is made for Bangladesh. It should understand our farming needs. Be easy to use for local farmers. We need a platform that helps farmers, in Bangladesh. The platform should support crops. It also needs to consider practices here. Language support is also important.

\section{Research Gap and Novelty of AgriGenie}

AgriGenie fills critical gaps in existing agricultural technology. For example:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Integrated Platform:} Combines disease detection, price prediction, marketplace access and real-time communication.
	\item \textbf{Localized Models:} Trained on crops and diseases relevant to Bangladesh.
	\item \textbf{Real-Time Communication:} Enables farmer–buyer interaction through WebSocket-based messaging.
	\item \textbf{Scalability:} Built on modern web technologies (Django, TensorFlow, Celery, Redis) for extensibility.
	\item \textbf{Role-Based Interfaces:} Provides customized dashboards for farmers, buyers and administrators.
	\item \textbf{Comprehensive Functionality:} Integrates disease diagnosis, price forecasting, weather alerts and marketplace transactions.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Methodology}

\section{System Architecture Overview}

AgriGenie is made up of parts that work together. It has a client and a server. The client is what the users see and interact with. The server is what does all the work behind the scenes. This makes AgriGenie easy to use and maintain.

The part that the users see is made with React.js and Bootstrap 5. This means that farmers and buyers and people who manage the system can all use it easily. They can see all the information they need on their computers or phones.

The server part is made with Django 4.2. This is what makes sure everything works properly. It is like the brain of AgriGenie. It makes sure people are who they say they are. It helps with things like managing crops and buying and selling.

All the information is stored in a database called PostgreSQL. This database is very organized. It has lots of sections for different types of information. There are over 38 sections.

AgriGenie also uses some tools to make it work better. It uses computer programs to help detect diseases in crops and to predict prices. It also uses a service that provides weather information. This means that users can get warnings if there is weather coming. AgriGenie can also send messages to users in time.

All these parts work together. They are like layers of a cake. Each layer can be changed without messing up other layers. This makes AgriGenie very flexible. There is a picture that shows how all these parts work together. It is called Figure~\ref{fig:system_architecture}. This picture shows how requests, from users go through the system and how all the parts work together.

\section{Database Design}

\subsection{Entity-Relationship Model}

The AgriGenie database is made to handle things that happen across many parts. The main part of the AgriGenie database is the \textbf{CustomUser} entity which takes care of who can do what and makes sure only the right people can get in. Farmers can add details to their profiles like how big their land is, what kind of crops they like to grow and how they want to get paid. Buyers can make lists of the crops they want and save the ones they like. All the crops that are for sale are connected to \textbf{MasterCrop} templates so that all the crops are put into the groups. When farmers and buyers leave reviews and ratings for each other it helps build trust between them. The AgriGenie database also helps farmers and buyers make decisions by giving them information about diseases that can hurt their crops and weather that might affect them.

\section{Main Parts Of AgriGenie}

\subsection{Finding Diseases In Plants}

The AgriGenie system has a way to detect diseases in plants, which uses a kind of computer model called ResNet-50. This model was trained using 15,000 pictures of plants and is very good at recognizing diseases. When a farmer wants to use this system, they upload a picture of a leaf or a plant. The system then prepares the picture for the model by making it the right size. The model then looks at the picture. Tries to figure out what disease the plant might have. It can recognize 15 diseases in plants like potatoes, tomatoes and peppers. If the model is not sure what the disease is, it does not make a guess. The results are shown to the farmer along with a score that says how sure the model is and some advice, on what to do to make the plant better. This system is connected to the marketplace so farmers can keep track of what diseases their plants have had and see trends.

\subsection{Price Prediction Algorithm}

The Price Prediction Algorithm is used to guess the prices of things like potatoes, tomatoes and rice. It uses something called Long Short-Term Memory networks to look at how prices change over time. The Price Prediction Algorithm looks at what happened to prices over the 10 years to make predictions. This helps it understand when prices might go up or down during times of the year.

The Price Prediction Algorithm can tell farmers what prices might be like for the 30 days. It can also say how much prices might change and when farmers should sell their crops like potato, tomato and rice. This helps farmers make plans, for when to harvest and sell their crops. The Price Prediction Algorithm is very helpful because it reduces the losses farmers have when prices change suddenly.
Price prediction utilizes LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) neural networks to capture temporal dependencies in agricultural commodity prices. The equation is:

$$P_t = LSTM(P_{t-1}, P_{t-2}, \ldots, P_{t-n}) + \epsilon$$

where $P_t$ represents predicted price at time $t$ and $\epsilon$ represents prediction error. Historical prices spanning 10 years are used to train the model, incorporating seasonal patterns and market trends.

\subsection{Recommendation System}
The AgriGenie marketplace has a recommendation system. This recommendation system uses filtering to make the experience better for buyers. It looks at what buyers like and what crops're like and then suggests listings that are a good match. The recommendation system is good for buyers because it helps them find what they want. It is also good for farmers because more people see what they have to sell. Buyers can review things, which helps build trust. AgriGenie looks at what buyers bought and what crops are like so buyers can find crops that are right for them. The recommendation system is a part of AgriGenie and it helps buyers and farmers in many ways.
The marketplace recommendation system employs collaborative filtering, computing similarity between buyer preferences and crop characteristics. The equation is:

$$\text{Similarity}(B, C) = \frac{\sum_{i} (r_{B,i} - \bar{r}_B)(w_{C,i} - \bar{w}_C)}{\sqrt{\sum_i (r_{B,i} - \bar{r}_B)^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_i (w_{C,i} - \bar{w}_C)^2}}$$

where $r_{B,i}$ represents buyer B's rating for crop category $i$, and $w_{C,i}$ represents weight of category $i$ for crop $C$.

	REST APIs enable modular communication between frontend and backend, ensuring scalability and easy integration with mobile apps.
	
	\item \textbf{Real-time: Django Channels with Daphne ASGI} \\
	Enables WebSocket-based communication for chat and notifications, ensuring farmers and buyers can interact instantly.
	
	\item \textbf{Task Queue: Celery with Redis broker} \\
	Handles background jobs such as weather alerts, price updates, and model retraining without blocking user requests.
	
	\item \textbf{Authentication: Django-allauth + OTP} \\
	Provides secure login with Google OAuth2 and custom OTP verification, ensuring multi-factor authentication for farmers.
	
	\item \textbf{Payment Gateway: SSLCommerz Integration} \\
	Ensures secure online transactions with dual payment methods (Cash on Delivery and Online Payment).

\chapter{Complex Engineering Problems and Solutions}

\section{Database Schema Compatibility Across Platforms}
During development, AgriGenie needed to support both PostgreSQL for production and SQLite for local testing. Migration failures occurred due to vendor-specific SQL syntax, such as PostgreSQL’s “IF NOT EXISTS” clause and boolean literal differences. To resolve this, vendor-aware migrations were implemented, allowing the system to detect the database backend and apply appropriate SQL logic. Backend-aware settings were also introduced to conditionally configure database options. These solutions ensured smooth compatibility across platforms and highlighted the importance of database-agnostic design in scalable systems.

\section{Machine Learning Model Serving at Scale}
The disease detection model initially caused long response times when invoked directly from Django views, as each request required model loading and blocked database connections. To address this, inference was moved to asynchronous Celery tasks, allowing users to receive immediate feedback while computations occurred in the background. Model caching eliminated repeated loading, and batch processing improved GPU utilization. As a result, API response times dropped to 200ms, GPU efficiency increased to 75\%, and user experience was significantly enhanced.

\section{Real-time Messaging System Scalability}
AgriGenie’s real-time messaging system faced scalability challenges when using Django Channels, as each WebSocket connection consumed excessive memory. With over 100 simultaneous connections, server resources were quickly exhausted. The solution involved implementing Redis-backed channel layers, which distributed message broadcasting across processes. Database persistence was handled asynchronously to avoid blocking. This architecture reduced memory usage per connection, supported over 50 concurrent users, and achieved message delivery latency of 100–200ms, ensuring reliable real-time communication.

\section{Price Prediction Model Accuracy}
The initial LSTM-based price prediction model performed well on training data but failed to generalize to new market conditions, primarily due to overfitting and limited feature engineering. To overcome this, an ensemble approach was adopted, combining LSTM for temporal dependencies with regression models that incorporated external features such as rainfall and commodity indices. Predictions were generated using a weighted average of both models, reducing the mean absolute percentage error to 7.3\% and narrowing the generalization gap from 15\% to 3\%. This approach improved robustness and reliability in real-world scenarios.

\section{Cross-Origin Resource Sharing (CORS) and Security}
Because the frontend (React) and backend (Django) operated on different domains, secure CORS configuration was essential. Overly permissive settings initially introduced vulnerabilities. The solution involved implementing environment-specific CORS policies, restricting allowed origins to trusted domains, and enforcing CSRF token validation on state-modifying requests. SameSite cookie policies were applied in production to prevent cross-site request forgery. These measures ensured secure communication between frontend and backend while maintaining usability.

\section{Limitations}
Despite its strengths, AgriGenie has certain limitations that highlight areas for future improvement:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Offline Functionality:} The platform currently requires continuous internet connectivity to function. This poses challenges for farmers in rural areas where network coverage is poor or unstable. An offline-first design, with synchronization once connectivity is restored, would significantly improve usability and adoption in such regions.
	
	\item \textbf{Language Support:} At present, the interface is available only in English. While this works for some users, it limits accessibility for the majority of farmers in Bangladesh who are more comfortable with Bengali. Incorporating Bengali translation and localized agricultural terminology is essential to broaden reach and inclusivity.
	
	\item \textbf{Model Coverage:} The disease detection module currently supports five major crops. Although this covers staple crops, many farmers cultivate a wider variety of vegetables, fruits, and spices. Expanding the model’s coverage will require additional training data and validation to ensure accuracy across diverse crop types.
	
	\item \textbf{Geographic Limitations:} Price prediction data is presently sourced from a limited set of markets. This restricts the accuracy of forecasts for farmers in regions not represented in the dataset. Extending coverage to include national-level market data would enhance prediction reliability and provide more comprehensive insights.
	
	\item \textbf{Payment Integration:} The marketplace relies on external payment gateways for transaction completion. While functional, this creates dependency and may not fully align with local practices. Deeper integration with widely used mobile money services such as bKash and Nagad would make transactions more seamless and farmer-friendly.
	
	\item \textbf{Quality Assurance:} Current marketplace verification mechanisms are relatively basic, focusing mainly on user approval and crop listing moderation. More sophisticated validation processes, such as automated fraud detection and quality certification, are needed to strengthen trust and ensure product authenticity.
	
	\item \textbf{Scalability Testing:} The system has been validated to support 500 concurrent users, which demonstrates good scalability for initial deployment. However, larger-scale deployments will require extensive stress testing to ensure performance stability under thousands of simultaneous users, particularly during peak agricultural seasons.
\end{enumerate}
